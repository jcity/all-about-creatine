Creatine is the most well-researched and effective sports supplement available today. Whether you're an athlete looking to improve performance or simply someone interested in the science behind this compound, this guide covers everything you need to know.

## What Is Creatine?

Creatine is a naturally occurring compound found in your muscles and brain. Your body produces about 1-2 grams of creatine per day from amino acids (glycine, arginine, and methionine), primarily in your liver and kidneys.

You also get creatine from dietary sources — mainly red meat and fish. A pound of raw beef or salmon contains roughly 1-2 grams of creatine.

<Callout type="info" title="Key Fact">
Your body stores about 95% of its creatine in skeletal muscles, with the remaining 5% found in the brain, kidneys, and liver.
</Callout>

## How Does Creatine Work?

Creatine's primary role is in the **phosphocreatine (PCr) energy system** — your body's fastest way to regenerate ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the universal energy currency of your cells.

Here's the simplified process:

1. During high-intensity exercise, your muscles rapidly break down ATP for energy
2. ATP loses a phosphate group and becomes ADP (adenosine diphosphate)
3. Phosphocreatine donates its phosphate group to ADP, regenerating ATP
4. This allows you to sustain high-intensity effort for a few more seconds

By supplementing with creatine, you increase your muscle's phosphocreatine stores by approximately 20-40%. This means more available fuel for short bursts of intense activity.

## Benefits of Creatine

Research has consistently demonstrated several well-established benefits:

### Strength and Power
Creatine supplementation typically increases strength performance by 5-15%. This is particularly noticeable in compound movements like squats, bench press, and deadlifts.

### Muscle Growth
By enabling you to perform more total work in your training sessions, creatine indirectly supports muscle hypertrophy. Some research also suggests creatine may directly influence cell signaling pathways involved in muscle growth.

### Exercise Performance
Creatine is most effective for short-duration, high-intensity activities — think sprinting, jumping, and resistance training. It's less impactful for pure endurance activities.

### Brain Health
Emerging research suggests creatine may support cognitive function, particularly under conditions of stress, sleep deprivation, or aging. Your brain is metabolically active and relies on phosphocreatine for energy.

<Callout type="tip" title="Who Benefits Most?">
Creatine tends to be most effective for people who have lower baseline creatine levels — including vegetarians and vegans, who don't get dietary creatine from meat and fish.
</Callout>

## Types of Creatine

While there are many forms of creatine on the market, not all are created equal:

| Type | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Creatine Monohydrate | Strong | Gold standard, most researched |
| Micronized Creatine | Strong | Finer particle monohydrate, dissolves better |
| Creatine HCl | Moderate | More soluble, may cause less bloating |
| Buffered Creatine | Weak | No proven advantage over monohydrate |
| Creatine Ethyl Ester | Weak | Actually less effective than monohydrate |
| Liquid Creatine | Weak | Degrades in liquid form over time |

## How to Take Creatine

### Daily Dose
The standard maintenance dose is **3-5 grams per day**. This is sufficient to fully saturate your muscle creatine stores within 3-4 weeks.

### Loading Phase (Optional)
Some people choose to "load" creatine by taking 20 grams per day (split into 4 doses of 5g) for 5-7 days. This saturates your muscles faster but isn't necessary — you'll reach the same saturation level with 3-5g daily, it just takes a few weeks longer.

### Timing
Creatine timing doesn't matter much. Take it whenever it's convenient and consistent. Some research suggests a slight benefit to taking it post-workout with a meal, but the difference is minimal.

### With What?
Creatine can be mixed with water, juice, or a protein shake. Taking it with carbohydrates may slightly improve uptake due to insulin-mediated transport.

## Safety and Side Effects

Creatine is one of the most thoroughly studied supplements in existence, with research spanning over three decades.

### Is Creatine Safe?
**Yes.** The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) has concluded that creatine monohydrate is safe for healthy individuals when used at recommended doses. Long-term studies (up to 5 years) have shown no adverse health effects.

### Common Concerns Addressed

- **Kidney damage** — No evidence of kidney harm in healthy individuals. People with pre-existing kidney conditions should consult their doctor.
- **Dehydration** — Creatine causes muscles to retain water intracellularly, but it does NOT cause dehydration. Stay normally hydrated.
- **Hair loss** — Based on a single study with methodological limitations. The bulk of evidence does not support this claim.
- **Weight gain** — You may gain 2-4 pounds of water weight initially. This is intracellular water in your muscles, not fat.

<Callout type="warning" title="Medical Note">
If you have pre-existing kidney disease or are taking medications that affect kidney function, consult your healthcare provider before supplementing with creatine.
</Callout>

## Who Should Take Creatine?

Creatine is beneficial for a wide range of people:

- **Athletes and gym-goers** looking to improve strength and performance
- **Vegetarians and vegans** who don't get dietary creatine
- **Older adults** who want to preserve muscle mass and cognitive function
- **Anyone** interested in the well-documented cognitive benefits
