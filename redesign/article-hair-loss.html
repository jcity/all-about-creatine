Does Creatine Cause Hair Loss?

The claim traces back to a single 2009 study. Here's what that study actually measured — and what three decades of research say about creatine and your hairline.

ACAll About Creatine Editorial Team Reviewed by Dr. Sarah Reyes, RD, PhD 9 min read · Jun 2026
Illustrative — the hair-loss concern centers on the hormone DHT, not creatine itself.

Key takeaways

  • The hair-loss worry comes from one 2009 study of 20 rugby players that measured a hormone (DHT) — not actual hair loss.
  • No study has ever shown creatine directly causes hair to fall out.
  • That single result has not been replicated in the 15 years since.
  • If you're already prone to male-pattern baldness, talk to a doctor — but the evidence linking creatine to hair loss is weak.

It's one of the most common worries about creatine, and it spreads fast on social media: take creatine, lose your hair. The concern isn't completely invented — it has a specific origin in the research literature. But when you trace it back to the source, the story is far less alarming than the headlines suggest.

Where the claim comes from

The entire creatine–hair loss idea rests on a single 2009 study conducted in college-aged rugby players.1 Over three weeks, the 20 participants who took creatine showed an increase in dihydrotestosterone (DHT) — a hormone derived from testosterone.

Why does that matter? Because DHT is strongly linked to male-pattern baldness in people who are genetically susceptible. DHT binds to receptors in hair follicles and, in those with the genetic predisposition, gradually shrinks them.2 So the logic became: creatine raises DHT, DHT causes baldness, therefore creatine causes hair loss.

The study measured a hormone associated with hair loss — it never measured hair loss itself.

The problem with that logic

There are three important gaps between that study and the scary conclusion:

1. It measured a marker, not an outcome

The researchers tracked DHT levels in the blood. No one in the study actually lost hair — or rather, hair loss was never measured at all. A change in a hormone is not the same as a change in your hairline.

2. The DHT increase stayed within normal range

While DHT rose relative to baseline, the values largely remained inside the normal physiological range. The increase was also reported as a ratio, which can amplify how dramatic a change appears.

3. It has never been replicated

This is the big one. In the 15+ years since, no other study has reproduced the finding — and a number of subsequent studies measuring testosterone and related hormones have found no meaningful effect from creatine.3 In science, a single unreplicated result is a starting point for more research, not a settled conclusion.

In plain terms

One small study found a hormone went up. It didn't find hair falling out, the change stayed mostly normal, and no one has been able to repeat it since. That's a thin foundation for a widespread fear.

What the broader evidence says

Creatine monohydrate is one of the most studied supplements in existence, with research spanning more than three decades.4 Across that body of work, hair loss has not emerged as a documented side effect. The International Society of Sports Nutrition's position stand on creatine — a comprehensive review of the safety literature — does not list hair loss among the established effects.4

So should you worry?

For most people, the evidence linking creatine to hair loss is too weak to justify avoiding a supplement with such well-documented benefits. That said, a sensible, honest answer acknowledges nuance:

  • If you have no family history of baldness: the concern is largely theoretical, and the current evidence doesn't support it.
  • If you're already experiencing male-pattern hair loss or have a strong family history: it's reasonable to discuss it with a dermatologist or your doctor, who can weigh your individual situation.
  • If you do start creatine and notice changes: remember that hair shedding has many causes (stress, diet, genetics, season). Correlation isn't causation — but a professional can help you sort it out.

The bottom line

The creatine–hair loss connection is built on a single, unreplicated study that measured a hormone, not hair. The much larger body of evidence shows creatine to be safe and effective. If you're genetically prone to baldness it's worth a conversation with your doctor — but for most people, this is a fear that the science simply doesn't support.
